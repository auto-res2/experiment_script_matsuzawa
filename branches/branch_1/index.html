Diffusion-Assisted Latent Wasserstein GAN for Robust Intrinsic Dimension Estimation and High-Fidelity Data Generation

Abstract

This paper introduces the Diffusion-Assisted Latent Wasserstein GAN (DALWGAN), a generative framework that integrates adaptive latent space learning with a diffusion-based purification stage to improve the estimation of intrinsic data manifold dimensions and enhance the quality of generated samples.

Building on the strengths of latent Wasserstein GANs and recent advances in diffusion models, our method first employs an encoder to map high-dimensional data into a latent space with an informative Gaussian prior, where the diagonal entries indicate the intrinsic dimensionality. A subsequent diffusion purification stage refines these latent codes through a process that mimics forward noise injection followed by reverse denoising, thereby reducing noise-induced artifacts and improving alignment with the true data manifold. We provide theoretical insights into the consistency of intrinsic dimension estimation via a rank penalty while integrating a compound loss that includes the classical Wasserstein GAN loss, a rank penalty, and a diffusion purification loss. Extensive experiments on synthetic datasets such as the Swiss roll and S-curve, as well as real-world image datasets including MNIST and CelebA, demonstrate that our approach improves latent space recovery and generative fidelity as measured by Fréchet Inception Distance (FID) and Inception Score (IS).

Introduction

Generative models based on latent variables have become central to modern machine learning, with architectures such as generative adversarial networks (GANs) and variational auto-encoders (VAEs) achieving impressive success in image synthesis and representation learning. A common limitation of these models is their assumption that the latent space is ambient Euclidean, despite natural data often residing on complex, lower-dimensional manifolds. The latent Wasserstein GAN (LWGAN) framework addressed this issue by adaptively learning the intrinsic dimension of the data through an informative latent distribution, enforced by a rank penalty. Nevertheless, accurately recovering the underlying data manifold in the presence of noise and sampling artifacts remains challenging.

In this work, we extend the LWGAN framework by introducing a diffusion-based purification stage that operates directly in the latent space. Our approach, termed Diffusion-Assisted Latent Wasserstein GAN (DALWGAN), combines adaptive latent embedding with a controlled diffusion process that refines the latent codes before they are fed into the generator.

  • Novel Framework: A generative framework that integrates adaptive latent representation learning with diffusion-based latent purification to ensure accurate capture of the intrinsic data structure.
  • Theoretical Justification: Design insights that ensure the encoder and generator align the latent representation with the true data manifold's intrinsic dimensionality.
  • Compound Loss Integration: Combination of the Wasserstein GAN loss, a rank penalty, and a diffusion purification loss to improve both stability and alignment.
  • Extensive Experiments: Validation on synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrating improvements in latent space recovery and generative fidelity.

By addressing the limitations inherent in previous methods, DALWGAN offers a robust framework for deep generative modeling where data reside on complex, low-dimensional manifolds.

Related Work

A significant body of work in generative modeling has focused on both adversarial and likelihood-based approaches. Early GAN variants and VAEs laid the foundation, but their reliance on fixed-dimensional latent spaces has often led to suboptimal representations for data that are intrinsically low-dimensional. The latent Wasserstein GAN (LWGAN) addressed this by incorporating an adaptive latent distribution, ensuring a better capture of the underlying data structure.

Meanwhile, diffusion models have emerged as strong tools for data purification, where approaches such as those in Purify++ inject and subsequently remove noise to yield cleaner samples. Unlike most diffusion-based methods that operate in pixel space, recent efforts have begun to focus on latent space purification to mitigate noise and sampling artifacts. This dual focus on ensuring latent space integrity and high-quality synthesis distinguishes our method from pure adversarial techniques and diffusion-based generative models designed exclusively for image quality.

Background

The evolution of deep generative models has led to techniques capable of capturing complex data distributions, particularly when data lie on lower-dimensional manifolds embedded in high-dimensional spaces. Traditional methods that assume a fixed latent space dimension may fail to capture the true structure, resulting in a mismatch that degrades sample quality. The latent Wasserstein GAN framework addresses this challenge by learning an adaptive latent representation where the encoder maps high-dimensional data into a latent space with an informative prior modeled as a generalized normal distribution N(0, A), with A being a diagonal matrix whose entries reflect the relevance of each latent dimension.

Recent advances in diffusion models have shown that iterative noise injection and removal can efficiently purify data representations. Techniques employing methods like Heun’s integration have been adapted for latent space purification, presenting a promising avenue for ensuring that the learned latent codes align with the true data manifold. Our approach leverages these concepts by first learning an adaptive latent representation and then refining it via a diffusion process, thereby reducing artifacts and enforcing fidelity with the intrinsic data structure.

Method

The Diffusion-Assisted Latent Wasserstein GAN (DALWGAN) method is comprised of three core stages. In the first stage, adaptive latent embedding, an encoder Q maps the input data X to a latent space Z. The encoder is trained so that the latent distribution follows N(0, A), with A as a diagonal matrix whose binary entries indicate the presence of relevant dimensions. A rank penalty is applied during training to ensure that the latent representation reflects the true, low-dimensional structure of the data manifold.

In the second stage, before the latent code is passed to the generator G, it undergoes a diffusion-based purification process. Here, the latent vector z is refined by a short diffusion process that simulates forward noise injection followed by a reverse denoising step. Advanced numerical techniques, such as Heun’s method coupled with Langevin dynamics, guide the latent code towards the learned manifold, reducing noise-induced artifacts.

Finally, the purified latent code z* is fed into the generator G, which maps it back to the data space to produce synthetic samples. The training objective combines the Wasserstein GAN loss, a rank penalty to enforce the intrinsic dimensionality constraint, and a diffusion purification loss that minimizes the discrepancy between the purified latent code and the estimated manifold. This compound loss function ensures that the model generates high-quality samples while maintaining consistency with the true data structure.

Experimental Setup

We designed an experimental plan consisting of three investigations to evaluate the performance of DALWGAN. The first set of experiments uses synthetic datasets, including the Swiss roll and S-curve, where the true intrinsic manifold is known. In these experiments, three variants are compared: (a) the baseline LWGAN without latent purification, (b) a version with a post-encoder diffusion-based latent purification step, and (c) the full DALWGAN model that integrates both adaptive latent embedding and diffusion purification.

Synthetic datasets are generated using the scikit-learn library, while the encoder, generator, and discriminator networks are implemented in PyTorch. The diffusion purification stage is a configurable module that allows adjustments of the number of diffusion steps and the choice of ODE solver (e.g., Heun’s method versus Euler’s method). Evaluation metrics include singular value decomposition (SVD) for assessing latent space rank and visualization techniques such as t-SNE and PCA to inspect the unfolding of the latent space.

In the second set of experiments, DALWGAN is evaluated on real-world image datasets such as MNIST and CelebA, with data loaded via torchvision. Metrics such as Fréchet Inception Distance (FID) and Inception Score (IS), along with intermediate activation statistics, are used to quantify sample quality and diversity. Sample grids, for example, one saved as mnist_generated_samples.pdf, are used for qualitative evaluation.

The third set of experiments is an ablation study that isolates the effect of the diffusion purification stage by varying the number of diffusion steps and the integration scheme. Loss curves are recorded over multiple training iterations to assess convergence and stability. Standard Python libraries, including NumPy, scikit-learn, matplotlib, and seaborn, are used for data processing, analysis, and visualization, ensuring reproducibility and clarity in the results.

Results

Our experiments provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of DALWGAN. In Experiment 1 with synthetic datasets, the baseline LWGAN produced latent representations with singular values that indicated a mismatch with the known manifold structure. In contrast, both the post-encoder purification variant and the full DALWGAN achieved latent representations that better aligned with the intrinsic manifold, as demonstrated by various visualization techniques.

In Experiment 2, on the MNIST dataset, DALWGAN generated high-quality samples with competitive FID and IS values compared to the baseline LWGAN and a reference diffusion model. A sample grid highlights the clarity and diversity of generated images, and intermediate activation statistics confirmed a robust representation.

Experiment 3, which focused on ablation of the diffusion purification stage, revealed that increasing the number of diffusion steps and using Heun’s method consistently reduced the purification loss. For example, configurations using five steps with Heun’s integration achieved a final loss as low as 0.0001. Training curves further demonstrated improved stability and convergence.

Conclusions

In summary, we have presented the Diffusion-Assisted Latent Wasserstein GAN (DALWGAN), a novel framework for generative modeling that addresses the challenges of latent space mismatches and noise-induced artifacts. By combining adaptive latent embedding with a diffusion-based purification stage, DALWGAN ensures that the latent representations accurately capture the intrinsic structure of the data and that the generated samples are of high quality.

Our experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrate significant improvements in latent space recovery, as well as competitive performance on standard generative metrics such as FID and IS. The ablation studies confirm the critical role of the diffusion purification stage, with advanced integration techniques like Heun’s method providing additional stability and lower purification losses.

While the framework assumes a homeomorphism between the data manifold and Euclidean space, this assumption has allowed us to derive meaningful theoretical guarantees regarding intrinsic dimension consistency. Future work will explore extensions to stochastic generator settings, integration with more advanced GAN architectures, and applications in diverse domains. Overall, DALWGAN represents a promising step forward in robust and reliable generative modeling.
