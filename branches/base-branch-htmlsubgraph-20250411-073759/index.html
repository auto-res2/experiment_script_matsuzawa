Diffusion-Assisted Latent Wasserstein GAN for Robust Generative Modeling

Abstract

We propose the Diffusion-Assisted Latent Wasserstein GAN (DALWGAN), an advanced framework that enhances adaptive latent space learning by integrating a diffusion-based purification stage directly into the latent representation. Building on the LWGAN approach, which fuses a Wasserstein auto-encoder with a Wasserstein GAN to estimate the intrinsic data manifold, DALWGAN refines latent codes through a brief diffusion process that injects controlled noise followed by a reverse denoising procedure using methods such as Heun’s integration.

This additional purification step mitigates latent mismatches and sampling artifacts, thereby improving both the stability and fidelity of generated samples. Comprehensive experiments on synthetic datasets (e.g., Swiss roll) and real-world datasets (e.g., MNIST, CelebA) demonstrate that our method produces latent representations that more accurately capture the true intrinsic dimension, as evidenced by singular value decomposition analyses and visualizations (PCA and t-SNE). Quantitative evaluations using metrics like Fréchet Inception Distance (FID) and Inception Score (IS) further validate the benefits of integrating diffusion purification. Overall, DALWGAN provides a principled and effective approach for robust generative modeling.

Introduction

Recent advances in deep generative modeling have underscored the importance of learning latent representations that accurately reflect the intrinsic structure of data. Traditional approaches such as GANs and VAEs often assume that a simple Gaussian prior is sufficient to model data variability; however, many real-world datasets reside on low-dimensional manifolds embedded in high-dimensional spaces. LWGAN addresses this misalignment by adaptively learning the intrinsic dimension through a modified latent distribution parameterized by a generalized normal distribution, where a diagonal covariance matrix plays a critical role in enforcing the correct dimensionality.

In practice, noise and model imperfections can lead to latent codes that are misaligned with the true data manifold. In response, we introduce DALWGAN, which augments the LWGAN framework with an integrated diffusion-based latent purification stage. This stage, implemented between the encoder and generator, refines latent codes by first injecting noise and then applying a controlled reverse diffusion process to denoise and align the representation with the true manifold.

Our contributions are as follows:

  • Adaptive Latent Purification: We extend adaptive latent space learning by integrating a diffusion purification stage that refines latent representations.
  • Compound Loss Function: We propose a compound loss function that includes the Wasserstein GAN loss, a rank penalty to enforce intrinsic dimensionality, and a purification loss that aligns latent codes with the target manifold.
  • Theoretical Insights: We provide theoretical insights regarding stability improvements and an upper bound on the generalization error.
  • Empirical Validation: We validate our approach through extensive experiments on synthetic and real datasets, using SVD analyses, PCA and t-SNE visualizations, and standard generative metrics.

Related Work

Recent research in generative modeling has focused on capturing the underlying structure of data through improved latent representations. Traditional GANs and their variants have introduced various constraints to enhance training stability and output quality. The LWGAN framework innovates by combining a Wasserstein auto-encoder with a GAN objective to preserve the intrinsic dimensionality of the data via a rank penalty.

Parallel to these developments, diffusion-based methods, as implemented in recent works like Purify++, have shown effectiveness in noise reduction and image purification, typically operating in the pixel space. In contrast, our method applies diffusion in the latent space, where refining the representation directly contributes to improved generative quality. DALWGAN bridges the gap between these ideas by first learning an informative latent embedding and then applying a diffusion purification process to correct latent mismatches. Unlike methods that focus solely on pixel-level fidelity, our approach enforces a rank penalty to capture true intrinsic dimensionality and incorporates the diffusion process as part of the training loss, resulting in a more stable and robust generative model. Comparative evaluations against baseline LWGAN and state-of-the-art diffusion models highlight the distinct advantage of our integrated approach.

Background

Latent variable models and manifold learning form the foundation of modern generative modeling. Standard GANs typically assume that a simple Gaussian prior is adequate for representing complex data variability. However, empirical evidence suggests that many datasets, such as natural images, lie on structured, low-dimensional manifolds.

The LWGAN framework addresses this by adopting an informative latent distribution, modeled as a generalized normal distribution N(0, A), where A is a diagonal matrix whose binary entries indicate the effective latent dimensions. The encoder network maps input data to latent codes, while the generator network reconstructs data from these codes. A key innovation in LWGAN is the application of a rank penalty during training, which encourages the learned latent space to reflect the true intrinsic dimensionality.

Concurrently, diffusion-based methods have emerged as powerful tools for denoising and image purification by employing a forward process to inject noise and a reverse process to recover clean data. By leveraging these ideas, our method applies diffusion directly in the latent space—an approach that promises to correct for subtle misalignments between learned representations and the data manifold.

This background establishes the context for our proposal, which integrates adaptive latent embedding with a diffusion-based purification module to enhance both reconstruction quality and sample fidelity.

Method

The Diffusion-Assisted Latent Wasserstein GAN (DALWGAN) builds on the LWGAN framework by incorporating a latent purification stage based on diffusion processes. The method consists of three primary steps. First, an encoder Q maps input data X to a latent code Z that follows an informative prior modeled as N(0, A), where A is a diagonal matrix with binary entries that capture intrinsic dimensionality. A rank penalty is enforced on A during training to ensure that the latent space accurately reflects the low-dimensional structure of the data.

Second, instead of feeding the raw latent code directly into the generator G, we introduce a diffusion-based purification module. This module first applies a forward diffusion step that injects controlled noise into the latent code, followed by a reverse denoising process implemented using numerical techniques such as Heun’s method. The reverse process is designed to optimally control noise via a combination of purification ordinary differential equations and Langevin dynamics, resulting in a refined latent code denoted as z*.

Finally, the purified latent code z* is passed to the generator G, which synthesizes data samples. The overall training objective is a compound loss function that comprises the traditional Wasserstein GAN loss (ensuring that the generated data distribution approximates the real distribution), a rank penalty loss to maintain the intrinsic dimensionality constraint, and a diffusion purification loss that minimizes the discrepancy between z* and the target manifold representation. The training process alternates between optimizing the encoder, the diffusion purification module, and the generator, ensuring that the latent embedding remains both informative and robust. By operating in the latent space rather than directly in the pixel space, our approach achieves computational efficiency while preserving the benefits of diffusion-based denoising.

Experimental Setup

Our experimental evaluation is structured around three key investigations.

Intrinsic Dimension Recovery: We assess intrinsic dimension recovery on synthetic datasets such as the Swiss roll, generated using scikit-learn. The encoder, generator, and diffusion purification modules are implemented in PyTorch. We compare three variants:

  • Baseline LWGAN: Without purification.
  • Post-Encoder Purification: LWGAN with a post-encoder diffusion purification stage.
  • Full DALWGAN: The complete model with integrated diffusion purification.

Performance metrics include singular value decomposition (SVD) of the latent codes and visualizations using PCA and t-SNE; corresponding plots are saved as singular_values.pdf, latent_pca.pdf, and latent_tsne.pdf.

Generative Quality Evaluation: We evaluate generative quality on real-world datasets using MNIST and CelebA, with data loaded via torchvision. A simple generator network creates synthetic images, and models are trained using standard optimizers. Qualitative assessments, such as sample grids (mnist_generated_samples.pdf), and quantitative metrics including FID and Inception Score (IS) are used to gauge performance.

Ablation Study: An ablation study is performed by varying parameters in the diffusion purification module. By altering the number of diffusion steps (e.g., 5 versus 15) and comparing integration schemes (Euler versus Heun), we analyze the impact on training loss and latent representation quality. Training settings for the ablation study are controlled using a small synthetic dataset, and the results are visualized in the diffusion_ablation.pdf plot.

This comprehensive setup provides rigorous evidence for the benefits of latent purification in the DALWGAN framework.

Results

Experimental results confirm that integrating diffusion-based latent purification significantly enhances both latent representation quality and sample fidelity. In the synthetic data experiments, the SVD analysis of the baseline encoder outputs revealed singular values of approximately 669.63 and 31.84, indicating that two dominant dimensions capture most of the variance. Visualizations using PCA and t-SNE demonstrate that the latent representations obtained from the full DALWGAN are more unfolded and better aligned with the underlying manifold.

In real-world experiments on the MNIST dataset, the DALWGAN produced visually superior sample grids and achieved competitive FID and Inception Score results compared to both baseline LWGAN and a reference diffusion-based generative model.

Furthermore, the ablation study showed that configurations using Heun’s method with a lower number of diffusion steps (e.g., 5 steps) resulted in lower mean squared error losses compared to Euler-based schemes.

These findings support the claim that the integrated diffusion purification stage contributes to more robust latent embedding and improved generative performance.

Conclusions

In summary, we have introduced DALWGAN, a novel framework that combines adaptive latent space learning with a diffusion-based purification stage to address inherent challenges in generative modeling.

By refining latent codes through a controlled diffusion process, our method successfully mitigates mismatches between the learned representations and the true data manifold, thereby enhancing sample quality and stability. The proposed compound loss function, which integrates the Wasserstein GAN loss, a rank penalty, and a diffusion purification loss, ensures that generated outputs faithfully capture the underlying structure of the data.

Extensive experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets validate the effectiveness of the approach, with significant improvements observed in latent structure alignment, visual fidelity, and convergence stability. Future work may explore more complex generator architectures, incorporate stochastic elements, and extend the framework to high-resolution image synthesis. Overall, DALWGAN represents a promising advancement in robust generative modeling.
