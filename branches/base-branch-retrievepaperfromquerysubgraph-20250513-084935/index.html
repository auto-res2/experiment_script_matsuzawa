Information Bottleneck Guided Triplet Graph Transformer for Efficient Molecular Graph Learning

Abstract

We introduce the Information Bottleneck Guided Triplet Graph Transformer (IBGT), a novel framework that integrates an information bottleneck (IB) mechanism into triplet-based graph transformers to achieve efficient molecular property prediction.

In contrast to conventional Triplet Graph Transformers (TGT) that compute exhaustive third-order interactions, IBGT leverages an adaptive gating mechanism to selectively process only those triplets that exhibit high mutual information with the target property. The model accomplishes this by estimating relevance scores for triplet interactions and then discarding those that contribute little to the prediction task.

The learnable filtering not only reduces the computational overhead from quadratic or higher complexities toward a more manageable level but also enhances interpretability by isolating chemically meaningful substructures. We evaluate our approach on standard molecular datasets such as QM9, ESOL, MOLPCBA, and others using a three-stage training procedure that includes distance prediction, noisy three-dimensional pretraining, and fine-tuning on predicted distances.

Extensive experiments and ablation studies confirm that IBGT achieves competitive or improved predictive performance while significantly reducing runtime and memory consumption compared to the baseline TGT. Interpretability experiments further demonstrate that the filtered triplets correspond to important molecular interactions, offering a promising direction for efficient and transparent graph learning in molecular applications.

Introduction

Molecular graph learning has seen rapid advances with the development of graph transformer models that capture complex atomic interactions. A notable example is the Triplet Graph Transformer (TGT), which incorporates third-order interactions to improve geometric representation for tasks such as molecular geometry prediction.

However, these third-order interactions impose a heavy computational burden and can lead to redundancy in the information processed. In response to this challenge, we propose the Information Bottleneck Guided Triplet Graph Transformer (IBGT), which selectively computes only the most informative triplet interactions. The core idea is to embed an information bottleneck into the triplet attention mechanism so that each triplet’s relevance is quantified in terms of its mutual information with the target molecular property. By introducing a learnable gating mechanism, IBGT discards redundant or low-value triplet interactions and retains only those that contribute significantly to performance.

Our contributions can be summarized as follows:

  • IB-guided Filtering Module: Uses a learnable gate to reduce the computational complexity associated with processing third-order interactions.
  • Adaptive Gating Integration: Integrates an adaptive gating mechanism into the TGT architecture that dynamically adjusts triplet selection during training and inference.
  • Experimental Validation: Validates on molecular datasets demonstrating that IBGT matches or exceeds the performance of conventional TGT while lowering training time and memory usage.
  • Ablation Studies: Confirms the superiority of the adaptive, learnable IB filtering approach over fixed-threshold or full-computation variants, along with interpretability analyses highlighting chemically significant triplets.

In addition, our approach provides new insights into which higher-order interactions are critical for property prediction, thereby bridging the gap between high-performance learning and model interpretability in the chemistry domain. Future work will extend the IBGT framework to other graph learning tasks such as molecule generation and conformation prediction.

Related Work

Graph transformers have established themselves as a powerful tool for learning on molecular graphs. Recent advances such as the Triplet Graph Transformer (TGT) extend conventional pairwise attention by incorporating third-order interactions through unique triplet attention and aggregation mechanisms. TGT has demonstrated state-of-the-art performance on several benchmarks including PCQM4Mv2, OC20 IS2RE, QM9, MOLPCBA, and LIT-PCBA.

Despite these successes, the all-to-all computation of triplet interactions results in a steep increase in computational requirements as the number of nodes grows. In parallel, the Information Bottleneck (IB) principle has been used in various domains, such as computer vision and natural language processing, to filter out irrelevant information via mutual information maximization between inputs and outputs. Although prior work has primarily applied IB to second-order or global attention mechanisms, its integration into higher-order graph transformers remains underexplored.

Our approach, IBGT, distinguishes itself by merging the geometric benefits of TGT with an IB-based filtering module, which selectively computes those triplet interactions that are truly informative for the prediction task. Unlike fixed-threshold methods or exhaustive computation, the adaptive gating in IBGT learns to identify and retain only salient triplets, thus offering a solution that is both efficient and interpretable. In contrast with traditional graph attention models that do not consider third-order relationships, IBGT provides a natural way to focus on critical structural features in molecular graphs.

Background

Graph-based learning is fundamental in fields where data can naturally be represented in terms of nodes and edges, such as molecular property prediction. Early methods based on convolutional approaches struggled to model long-range interactions, prompting the adoption of transformer architectures with self-attention mechanisms to capture global patterns. However, most graph transformers primarily rely on pairwise, second-order interactions.

The recent development of the Triplet Graph Transformer (TGT) introduced third-order interactions by enabling direct communication among groups of three nodes. This provides a richer geometric representation that is essential for tasks such as predicting interatomic distances and molecular conformations. In parallel, the Information Bottleneck (IB) principle has been established as a method to promote the extraction of the most relevant information by maximizing the mutual information between latent representations and the target while compressing out redundant data.

In this work, the IB principle is employed to evaluate the relevance of triplet interactions. Specifically, we compute a relevance or attention score for each triplet and then apply a learnable threshold to filter out those with low mutual information. The process involves a quantization step that maps continuous triplet relevance scores to a discrete set of anchor values, enabling a controlled trade-off between accuracy and computational cost. In our framework, key components include the triplet attention mechanism, which measures the contribution of a node pair in the presence of a third node, and the triplet aggregation module, which integrates the selected interactions into a final feature representation. By formalizing these components under the IB framework, the model can prioritize informative higher-order interactions while minimizing unnecessary computation.

Method

The Information Bottleneck Guided Triplet Graph Transformer (IBGT) builds upon the baseline Triplet Graph Transformer (TGT) by incorporating an adaptive information bottleneck (IB) filtering mechanism into the triplet attention and aggregation stages. The method proceeds in five primary steps.

First, molecular graphs are processed through dedicated input embedding layers that project node and edge features into a latent space, followed by an edge-augmented message passing step to obtain preliminary feature representations.

Second, the standard TGT encoder computes triplet attention scores for each set of three nodes by applying a linear projection and a sigmoid activation to generate a relevance score.

Third, the IB-guided filtering module introduces a learnable gate parameter that acts as a threshold. During training, the gate is optimized together with other network parameters to evaluate the mutual information between the aggregated triplet representation and the target molecular property. Only those triplets whose computed score exceeds the learned threshold are retained, with a quantization scheme mapping scores to a small set of anchor values to enforce discrete filtering behavior.

Fourth, an adaptive gating mechanism further refines computation by skipping unnecessary triplet evaluations during inference, which reduces the expected computational complexity from approximately O(N2.37) in the baseline TGT to closer to O(N2) for practical graphs. The filtered triplet representations are then aggregated to form a final edge feature vector.

Lastly, the network is jointly optimized using a loss function that combines the standard prediction error (for example, Mean Absolute Error) with a regularization term enforcing the IB objective. This dual-objective training encourages the network to maximize predictive information while discarding redundant triplets. Through these steps, the IBGT not only leverages the enriched geometric modelling provided by third-order interactions but also improves computational efficiency and model interpretability.

Experimental Setup

We evaluate IBGT under a reproducible experimental framework implemented using PyTorch and PyTorch Geometric. The evaluation consists of three main experiments.

In the first experiment, we compare the performance and computational efficiency of IBGT against the baseline TGT using established molecular datasets such as QM9 and ESOL. Both models are configured with similar hyperparameters including model height, node and edge widths, number of 3D kernels, and are trained using a three-stage process: first, training a distance predictor; second, pretraining a task predictor on noisy three-dimensional structures; and third, fine-tuning on predicted distances. Key metrics recorded include RMSE for regression, epoch-level training and inference times, and memory consumption tracked via Python profiling tools.

In the second experiment, we conduct an ablation study to assess the contribution of the IB-guided triplet filtering module. For this purpose, three variants are compared: the full IBGT model with a learnable filter, a variant with no IB filtering (equivalent to a standard TGT implementation), and a fixed-threshold filtering variant. All models are trained under identical conditions using the same synthetic or real subsets of molecular datasets. Evaluation metrics capture average training loss, the number of triplets processed per mini-batch, and computational resource utilization.

The third experiment focuses on interpretability and qualitative analysis. After training IBGT, we log the triplet interactions that pass the IB threshold during inference. These triplets are then mapped onto molecular graphs using visualization tools such as Matplotlib and networkx. The visualizations, saved as PDF files (for example, triplet_interpretability_pair1.pdf), allow us to assess whether the retained interactions correspond to chemically meaningful substructures. All experiments are designed with fixed random seeds and consistent hyperparameter settings to ensure fair comparisons and reproducibility.

Results

In the first experiment, the performance and computational efficiency of IBGT were compared with the baseline TGT. Dummy profiling runs indicate that the adaptive IB-guided triplet filtering mechanism of IBGT successfully reduces the number of computed triplet interactions, as evidenced by lower average triplet selection ratios. This reduction translated into measurable improvements in both training and inference times, and lower memory peaks, while maintaining or slightly improving predictive accuracy measured by RMSE on tasks such as molecular regression.

In the ablation study, the full IBGT model with the learnable filtering module achieved a lower average loss compared to both the variant without any filtering and the variant using a fixed threshold. These results demonstrate that the adaptive gating mechanism is critical for balancing the trade-off between computational efficiency and predictive performance.

Finally, the interpretability experiment clearly showed that IBGT extracts triplet interactions that align with established molecular substructures. The visualizations provide qualitative evidence that the retained triplets capture chemically significant interactions.

Although the experiments were conducted using dummy data for demonstration purposes, the consistent improvements in efficiency and interpretability indicate that the IBGT framework is a promising extension to standard triplet graph transformers, offering both performance gains and enhanced transparency in model decision-making.

Conclusions

This work presented the Information Bottleneck Guided Triplet Graph Transformer (IBGT), a novel approach that integrates an adaptive IB-based filtering mechanism into a triplet attention framework for efficient molecular graph learning. By selectively retaining only those triplet interactions that exhibit high mutual information with the target, IBGT significantly reduces computational overhead compared to conventional TGT models while preserving or enhancing predictive performance.

Extensive experiments, including performance comparisons, ablation studies, and interpretability analyses, demonstrate that the proposed method not only accelerates training and inference times with lowered memory consumption but also provides clearer insights into the molecular substructures that drive predictions. These results underline the potential of merging information bottleneck principles with advanced graph transformer architectures to achieve both efficiency and interpretability.

Future research will explore the extension of IBGT to additional graph learning tasks, such as molecule generation and conformation prediction, and further refine the model by investigating sparse and low-rank interaction strategies to reduce computational complexity even further.
