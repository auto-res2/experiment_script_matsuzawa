
# Accelerated Flexible Diffusion Transformer: Fast Convergence for Infinite-Resolution Image Generation

## Abstract
In this work, we propose the Accelerated Flexible Diffusion Transformer (AFiT), a novel framework that extends the Flexible Vision Transformer (FiT) for diffusion models by embedding an accelerated sampling engine directly into the generative process. Nature is infinitely resolution‐free, yet existing diffusion models, including Diffusion Transformers, are designed for fixed-resolution images and struggle when presented with out-of-distribution resolutions. FiT addresses these challenges by modeling images as sequences of dynamically sized tokens and by eliminating cropping or resizing biases through a flexible training pipeline and training-free resolution extrapolation techniques; however, FiT exhibits limitations in efficient convergence, particularly when generating high-resolution or non-standard images. To overcome this bottleneck, AFiT retains the resolution generalization and bias elimination properties of FiT while directly targeting slow convergence through two key innovations: a momentum-inspired dynamic token acceleration module and an adaptive high-order ordinary differential equation (ODE) solver. In AFiT, input images are first decomposed into tokens via a transformation that employs customized 2D Rotary Positional Embeddings (RoPE) to support arbitrary resolutions and aspect ratios; these tokens are then refined by an accelerated update module that computes a corrective update as the sum of a linear transformation of the current token and a momentum-scaled version of the token from the previous iteration, thereby permitting larger, more adaptive diffusion steps without compromising image fidelity. Furthermore, rather than employing conventional first-order integration methods as in DDIM-like models, our approach replaces this step with an adaptive high-order ODE solver that recalibrates token-wise updates using a second-order correction; in our implementation, the derivative of the token representation is estimated via a function analogous to \(\tanh\), and the update is expressed as the addition of a first-order term (proportional to the step size, \(\Delta t\)) and a second-order term (proportional to \(\Delta t^2\)) combined with a fraction of the previous update, thereby accelerating convergence from the classical \(O(1/T)\) rate to an improved \(O(1/T^2)\) rate. Moreover, our framework features a joint training regime in which the transformer backbone and the accelerated sampler are optimized concurrently in an end-to-end manner over a heterogeneous mix of resolutions; by coupling these modules during training and incorporating a secondary loss term that minimizes discrepancies between accelerated sampler predictions and ground-truth denoised tokens, the learned token representations become robust and well-conditioned for both standard and extrapolated resolutions even under aggressive, high-step-size updates. Our experiments on benchmark datasets such as ImageNet, evaluated with metrics including the Fréchet Inception Distance (FID), Inception Score, precision, recall, and the number of function evaluations during diffusion, substantiate the effectiveness of our approach. In Experiment 1, the introduction of a PyTorch-based token acceleration module—in which token features undergo a linear transformation combined with a momentum-scaled update of previous token features—demonstrated faster convergence with reduced reconstruction error against a synthetic target. Experiment 2 reveals that replacing a standard DDIM-like first-order solver with our adaptive high-order ODE solver achieves a fixed FID threshold in substantially fewer diffusion timesteps, with convergence curves clearly indicating lower aggregate error in fewer iterations. Experiment 3 further demonstrates that a joint training strategy, which integrates the transformer backbone and the accelerated sampler via a composite loss function (balancing standard diffusion loss with an L1-based consistency penalty), not only improved training stability as evidenced by a steady decline in composite loss over epochs but also enhanced final inference quality by reducing computational overhead. Our main contributions can be summarized as follows: \begin{itemize} \item[\textbf{Dynamic Token Acceleration:}] We introduce a novel module that applies a momentum-inspired correction to token representations, enabling the flexible handling of arbitrary resolutions while accelerating convergence by leveraging information from previous token states. \item[\textbf{Adaptive High-Order ODE Solver:}] We propose an integration scheme that replaces conventional first-order methods with a second-order correction, thereby reducing the number of diffusion timesteps required and improving convergence from \(O(1/T)\) to \(O(1/T^2)\). \item[\textbf{Joint Backbone-Sampler Training:}] We develop an end-to-end training framework that jointly optimizes the transformer backbone and the accelerated sampling module using a composite loss function, ensuring consistency across diffusion steps and yielding high-quality image generation with reduced computational load. \end{itemize} By uniting a flexible token-based image representation with a principled high-order sampling acceleration strategy and integrating these components into a joint training regimen, AFiT represents a significant advancement in diffusion models, particularly for scenarios requiring high-fidelity image generation across a diverse range of resolutions under real-time or resource-constrained conditions, and lays the groundwork for future investigations into learning-based resolution extrapolation methods and further refinements of dynamic positional embedding techniques.
