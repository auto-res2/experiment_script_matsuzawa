## Abstract
We present Multimodal Efficient BTLM (MM‐BTLM), a novel framework that unifies the robust text generation capabilities of BTLM-3B-8K with complementary multimodal processing inspired by Megrez-Omni. Built upon BTLM-3B-8K—which, with only 3 billion parameters and trained on a meticulously cleaned and deduplicated 627 billion token SlimPajama dataset using variable context lengths of 2,048 and 8,192 tokens, achieves performance competitive with 7 billion parameter models while exhibiting excellent long-context reasoning—MM‐BTLM overcomes the inherent limitations of text-only inputs by integrating additional modalities such as images and audio. While BTLM-3B-8K excels in pure text generation and surpasses other 3B models by 2–5.5% across diverse downstream tasks, its design is intrinsically tailored for textual inputs and struggles with closed-book world knowledge tasks, non-English applications, and situations where supplementary non-textual information is critical. To address these challenges, MM‐BTLM augments the efficient autoregressive decoder with lightweight, modality-specific encoder modules. In our design, compact vision and audio encoders—drawing inspiration from efficient convolutional approaches, transformer-based modules, and audio processing techniques similar to those employed in Whisper—are pre-trained on large-scale modality-specific datasets and subsequently co-optimized with the text module. Their outputs are dynamically integrated into the text representation via cross-modality attention layers. A central innovation is the adaptive gating mechanism; mathematically, given a text embedding sequence \(T \in \mathbb{R}^{L \times d}\) and a modality-specific feature vector \(V \in \mathbb{R}^{d}\), the adaptive gating is defined as \(\tilde{V} = V \odot \sigma(WV + b)\), where \(\sigma\) denotes the sigmoid activation function, \(W\) and \(b\) are learnable parameters, and \(\odot\) represents element-wise multiplication. The gated feature \(\tilde{V}\) is then fused with \(T\) through a cross-attention mechanism that computes attention weights between text tokens and the additional modality features, yielding a refined multimodal representation that encapsulates complementary semantic cues. This adaptive context and modality balancing strategy dynamically weights non-textual signals according to input quality so that, for example, ambiguous textual descriptions can be augmented by visual cues and audio signals may compensate for degraded text or background noise. Our training protocol for MM‐BTLM is organized into two main stages. First, a joint pretraining phase leverages large-scale multimodal datasets spanning text, images, and audio while retaining the extended context lengths characteristic of BTLM-3B-8K. In a subsequent fine-tuning stage, the cross-attention fusion layers are further calibrated for specific downstream tasks—including closed-book world knowledge retrieval and multilingual instruction following—with additional measures implemented to mitigate training data biases and enhance multilingual performance. Another key consideration in our development is practical deployability. MM‐BTLM is engineered with an edge-optimized co-design philosophy and incorporates quantization techniques that reduce numerical precision to 4-bit, thereby considerably decreasing the memory footprint—requiring only 3GB of memory—and reducing inference compute by up to 2.5× compared to comparable 7B parameter models. This efficiency enables advanced language understanding and multimodal integration on mobile and edge devices. We validate our approach through three Python-implementable experiments. In the first experiment, a multimodal fusion and cross-attention ablation study—conducted on a task analogous to image captioning using datasets such as MS COCO—demonstrates that deactivating the vision and audio pathways leads to measurable degradations in BLEU and ROUGE scores as well as classification accuracy, which confirms the contribution of each additional modality. The second experiment evaluates the adaptive context and modality balancing mechanism by simulating variable input conditions where one or more modalities are masked or degraded; task-specific accuracy, F1-scores, and mean average precision (mAP) measurements confirm that the adaptive gating mechanism assigns higher weights to higher quality signals. Finally, the third experiment examines edge-device inference and quantization efficiency by employing PyTorch’s dynamic quantization tools; profiling with utilities such as cProfile and timeit indicates substantial improvements in inference latency and reductions in model size when comparing the quantized MM‐BTLM to its unquantized counterpart. In summary, by leveraging joint pretraining, task-specific fine-tuning, dynamic cross-attention integration, and state-of-the-art quantization techniques, MM‐BTLM represents a significant advancement over text-only systems and paves the way for accessible, interactive, and robust artificial intelligence capable of advanced long-context reasoning and multimodal integration on edge platforms.
