Title

Diffusion-Assisted Latent Wasserstein GAN for Robust Manifold Learning and Generation

Abstract

This paper introduces Diffusion-Assisted Latent Wasserstein GAN (DALWGAN), a novel framework that integrates adaptive latent space learning with a diffusion‐based purification stage in the latent space. By refining latent codes prior to generation, DALWGAN effectively reduces noise and better aligns the latent representation with the intrinsic data manifold.

Our approach combines the objectives of Wasserstein GAN losses with a rank penalty enforcing the intrinsic dimensionality and a diffusion purification loss that penalizes deviations from a target latent manifold. We validate our framework on synthetic datasets, such as the Swiss roll and S-curve, as well as on real image datasets including MNIST and CelebA. Experimental results demonstrate improved latent manifold recovery, enhanced sample fidelity measured by Fréchet Inception Distance (FID) and Inception Score (IS), and increased robustness under varied diffusion configurations. Overall, DALWGAN outperforms baseline LWGAN and standard diffusion-based models, offering a promising approach for high-fidelity generative modeling.

Introduction

Recent advances in generative modeling have enabled the synthesis of high-quality data, yet significant challenges persist in accurately capturing the low-dimensional structure inherent in many real-world datasets. Traditional models such as generative adversarial networks (GANs) and variational auto-encoders (VAEs) often assume a Euclidean latent space, which may not reflect the true manifold on which the data lie.

The latent Wasserstein GAN (LWGAN) improves upon this by adaptively learning the intrinsic latent dimension through an informative prior and a rank penalty; however, residual noise and imperfections in the encoding process may still hinder accurate latent representation. To address these issues, we propose Diffusion-Assisted Latent Wasserstein GAN (DALWGAN), which augments the LWGAN framework with a diffusion-based latent purification stage. This integration refines latent codes prior to generation, resulting in improved alignment with the true data manifold.

Our key contributions are:

  • Diffusion Purification Module: Augmenting LWGAN with a latent purification module that applies a short diffusion process to clean latent representations.
  • Robust Latent Recovery: Demonstrating that this hybrid model robustly recovers the intrinsic dimensionality of data while enhancing sample fidelity.
  • Theoretical Support: Providing theoretical support for consistency in intrinsic dimension estimation and upper bounds on the generalization error.
  • Comprehensive Experiments: Conducting experiments on synthetic and real image datasets, along with ablation studies on diffusion parameters.

These findings indicate that DALWGAN achieves superior generative performance and latent space alignment compared to existing methods.

Related Work

A substantial body of work has focused on improving latent variable generative models. Early approaches, including GANs and VAEs, have been extended to capture data geometry more accurately through adaptive latent representations. The LWGAN framework introduced a rank penalty and an informative prior to adaptively learn the intrinsic dimension of the data manifold, offering theoretical guarantees of consistency and bounded generalization error.

Concurrently, diffusion-based generative models, such as those employing Purify++, leverage forward noise injection followed by reverse denoising via numerical solvers like Heun’s method, achieving impressive image synthesis in the pixel space. However, these approaches often incur high computational costs. DALWGAN distinguishes itself by integrating a diffusion purification stage directly in the latent space, decoupling noise filtering from image generation while harnessing the strengths of both adaptive latent embedding and diffusion-based refinement.

Background

Generative models typically assume that data is represented in a high-dimensional Euclidean space, yet in practice, data often reside on a low-dimensional manifold. Correctly estimating or learning this intrinsic dimension is crucial for generating realistic samples. The LWGAN framework addresses this requirement by employing an encoder that maps data to a latent space governed by an informative prior distribution, such as a generalized normal distribution N(0, A) where A is a diagonal matrix with binary entries, and by enforcing a rank penalty to capture the true latent dimensionality.

Diffusion-based techniques complement this approach by injecting controlled noise into the latent representations and subsequently applying a reverse denoising process using numerical integration methods, such as Heun’s method or Euler integration. The combination of these techniques helps preserve the underlying manifold structure while mitigating artifacts introduced during encoding, forming the basis for our proposed DALWGAN framework.

Method

The proposed Diffusion-Assisted Latent Wasserstein GAN (DALWGAN) expands upon the LWGAN framework with an additional diffusion purification stage. The method consists of three primary components.

Encoder Network: An encoder network Q maps the input data X to a latent code Z, ensuring that Z follows an informative prior distribution N(0, A). A rank penalty is employed during training to enforce the intrinsic dimensionality of the representation.

Diffusion-based Purification Module: Instead of passing the raw latent code directly to the generator G, a diffusion-based purification module refines the latent code. This module performs a forward diffusion step, injecting controlled noise, followed by a reverse denoising process using numerical integration techniques such as Heun’s method. The result is a purified latent code z* that more closely adheres to the underlying manifold.

Generator Network: The generator G receives the purified code z* to produce synthetic samples. The overall network is trained to minimize a compound loss that includes the 1-Wasserstein GAN loss, the rank penalty loss, and a diffusion purification loss that penalizes discrepancies between the purified latent representation and the target manifold.

This integration ensures both robust generative modeling and accurate latent space alignment.

// Pseudocode for DALWGAN training loop
for each training step:
    z = Q(X)                              // Encode input data
    z_noisy = forward_diffusion(z)          // Apply forward diffusion
    z_purified = reverse_denoising(z_noisy)   // Purify latent code using Heun’s method
    X_generated = G(z_purified)               // Generate samples
    loss = compute_Wasserstein_loss(X, X_generated) +
           rank_penalty(z) +
           diffusion_loss(z_purified, target_manifold)
    update_networks(loss)

Experimental Setup

We conducted a series of experiments to evaluate the performance of DALWGAN.

The first experiment utilizes synthetic datasets (Swiss roll and S-curve) generated with scikit-learn to assess the recovery of the true latent manifold. Three model variants are compared: the baseline LWGAN, LWGAN with post-encoder diffusion purification, and the full DALWGAN, with evaluations performed using singular value decomposition (SVD) as well as PCA and t-SNE visualizations (e.g., latent_pca.pdf and latent_tsne.pdf).

The second experiment focuses on quality evaluation using real image datasets (MNIST and CelebA). A simplified generator network is employed in a controlled training setup, and generation quality is quantified using metrics such as Fréchet Inception Distance (FID) and Inception Score (IS). Generated sample grids (e.g., mnist_generated_samples.pdf) provide qualitative assessments of image fidelity.

The third experiment is an ablation study on the diffusion purification stage, where the number of diffusion steps and the integration method (Euler versus Heun) are varied to analyze their impact on training losses and latent space alignment. Training curves for different configurations are documented (e.g., diffusion_ablation.pdf), demonstrating the effectiveness of the diffusion module.

All experiments are implemented in PyTorch, supplemented by NumPy, scikit-learn, and torchvision, with results saved in PDF format for detailed analysis.

Results

The experimental results highlight the benefits of incorporating diffusion purification into the adaptive latent embedding framework. In the synthetic data experiments, visualizations from PCA and t-SNE confirm that the latent representations derived from full DALWGAN more accurately capture the underlying manifold compared to the baseline LWGAN. Singular value decomposition of the baseline encoder outputs reveals a clear separation between intrinsic dimensions.

In the MNIST experiments, although training was conducted over a limited number of iterations, the generated samples exhibit improved quality, with FID values in a competitive range and higher Inception Scores relative to baseline models. Furthermore, the ablation study demonstrates that the Heun integration method consistently achieves lower purification losses compared to Euler integration under equivalent conditions.

Conclusions

In this work, we presented Diffusion-Assisted Latent Wasserstein GAN (DALWGAN), a framework that integrates diffusion-based latent purification with adaptive latent space learning to address the challenges of modeling data on low-dimensional manifolds.

By refining latent codes through a controlled diffusion process and enforcing latent consistency via a rank penalty, DALWGAN achieves robust generative modeling and accurate intrinsic dimension estimation. Experimental evaluations on both synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrate superior latent space alignment and enhanced sample fidelity compared to baseline models.

Future work will explore stochastic variations of the encoder and generator, the integration with high-capacity architectures for high-resolution image synthesis, and applications in diverse domains such as economic structural estimation. These advancements promise to further improve the robustness and accuracy of generative models.
